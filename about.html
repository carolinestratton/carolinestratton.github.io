About Me

I am currently completing my doctoral work at the University of Texas School of Information. My work falls in the multidisciplinary field known as information and communications technology for development (ICT4D). In my research, I examine how ICT4D interventions, teaching people to use ICT in the name of development, relate or do not relate to outcomes of development. I talk to people involved in ICT4D to understand their perceptions and beliefs about what the use of ICT does or should do and what development means in the context of distinct interventions.

I have a specific interest in situating our understanding of ICT4D in theories about international development. The cumulative history of development theory and practice has identified multiple roles for technologies in the development of countries: a catalyst of the economy, modernizer of industry, enabler of scientific autonomy. Questions about what makes ICT different from technologies favored in previous decades interest me. I'm a nuclear engineer by training and I see intriguing parallels among the prestigious technologies highlighted in international development efforts throughout the history of technical assistance and development intervention.

I have used ethnographic methods and qualitative research techniques in my fieldwork in Latin America, drawing on Spanish language skills that date back to my time as a high school exchange student in Alajuela, Costa Rica. With the generous support of NSF grant IIS-1412924, I spent three months in Colombia and three months in Bolivia during 2015, after setting up five field sites across South America for a multi-year collaboration with Drs. Diane Bailey (my advisor), Paul Leonardi, and Carlos Rodríguez-Lluesma. The primary product of my 2015 fieldwork will be my dissertation, expected for defense in April 2017.
